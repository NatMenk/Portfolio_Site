01 — Overview

Project Overview

A structured CRM contact database is only as useful as the insight it can generate. This project transformed a raw list of 885 upstream oil and gas contacts into a fully classified, persona-mapped segmentation framework — built entirely from first principles using Python, Excel, and manual LinkedIn verification.

The work spanned four phases: data collection and title enrichment, dual-axis classification (seniority × function), persona development, and density analysis by account. The output is an actionable marketing intelligence layer enabling account-based targeting, persona-specific messaging, and strategic prioritisation across a 36-company operator universe.

Industry Context

The upstream oil and gas sector is technically dominated, relationship-driven, and seniority-conscious. Generic CRM segmentation fails here. This project required deep domain understanding to build classifications that reflect how decisions actually get made in this industry.

02 — The Challenge

The Problem

What Existed

  • A flat list of 885 contacts — names, companies, emails
  • ~17% of contacts had no job title recorded
  • No seniority classification or function categorisation
  • No way to distinguish a VP from an EIT
  • LinkedIn blocks automated scraping — enrichment non-trivial

What Was Needed

  • Seniority and function classification for every contact
  • Persona definitions grounded in real titles, not archetypes
  • Account-level density analysis for prioritisation
  • Tailored outreach strategies per segment
  • A reusable framework that scales as the database grows
03 — Methodology

How It Was Built

1
Data Collection and Title Enrichment

LinkedIn actively blocks automated retrieval, so a custom Python web scraping script was built using Google Search as the data source. A manual QC pass followed — individually verifying each contact on LinkedIn to confirm, correct, or supplement titles where automated results were ambiguous or missing.

2
Dual-Axis Classification

Each contact was classified across two independent dimensions: Seniority (9 tiers: C-Suite through Unknown) and Job Function (8 categories: Engineering, Geoscience, Data and Analytics, Business Dev and Commercial, and more). Classifications were derived programmatically from job title text and validated manually.

3
Persona Development

Six marketing personas were developed from the seniority-function cross-tabulation, informed by deep industry knowledge of the upstream oil and gas buyer journey. Each persona was named after the most statistically common first name in that segment — grounding them in real data rather than invented characters.

4
Density Analysis

For each persona, company-level density was calculated as both absolute contact count and as a percentage of each company's total footprint — transforming the persona framework from a database-level view into an account-prioritisation tool.

07 — Strategic Implications

What the Data Tells Us

  1. 01

    The database is technically rich but champion-dependent

    With 49% of titled contacts in Senior or Mid-Level bands, the winning go-to-market motion is champion-led: build credibility with Scott and Kim first, then warm-introduce to Greg. Cold VP outreach will underperform.

  2. 02

    17.4% of contacts are unclassified — a significant data asset

    154 contacts are unclassified due to missing title data. A targeted enrichment pass could unlock approximately 63 additional Technical Core contacts, 14 Decision Makers, and 13 Managers.

  3. 03

    Three companies dominate — concentration is a strategic risk

    Operator E (20.0%), Operator F (11.2%), and Operator G (10.3%) represent 42% of the database. Deliberate mid-market expansion into Operator C, Operator L, Operator M, and Operator N would reduce this risk.

  4. 04

    Digital transformation is measurable in the data

    69 Data and Analytics contacts (7.8%) is a material signal for this capital-heavy sector. Operator F's 13% DandA density is the second-highest of any company — suggesting deliberate digital investment.

  5. 05

    The Commercial Operator is underserved — a differentiation opportunity

    Daniel is the smallest named persona (56 contacts) but the most strategically differentiated. Operator O (55.6% Daniel density) is effectively a commercial-operator-first company.

08 — Recommendations

Action Priorities

  1. Prioritise champion-led selling

    Scott and Kim are the entry points. Build technical credibility with these segments first, then warm-introduce to Greg. Avoid cold VP outreach.

  2. Run an ABM program at Operator F, Operator D, and Operator B

    These three companies offer the best combination of contact volume, persona diversity, and density across multiple segments.

  3. Create dedicated Commercial Operator content

    Build one landing page, one case study, and one demo specifically for land and JV professionals. This is a blue ocean within the database.

  4. Enrich the 154 Unknown contacts

    A targeted enrichment pass via LinkedIn, ZoomInfo, or direct outreach could unlock ~63 additional Technical Core contacts.

  5. Expand mid-market coverage

    Operator C, Operator L, Operator M, and Operator N all have high persona density relative to their size. Adding 5–10 contacts at each would disproportionately increase Decision Maker and Manager counts.
