The Challenge

The company needed to generate qualified demo pipeline from US financial institutions — banks, capital markets firms, and credit intermediaries — for its Canadian oil and gas investment analytics platform. The challenge: reaching actual decision-makers inside organisations that are inundated with financial data vendor pitches.

LinkedIn was selected for its professional targeting depth. A three-creative test was launched on April 14, 2026 with a combined daily budget across an Audience Network + LinkedIn feed placement mix.

"A 1.99% CTR in B2B LinkedIn is roughly four times the industry benchmark of 0.4–0.6%. The targeting was clearly well-matched to the creative — but the data also revealed where significant efficiency was being left on the table."

The "Investing in Canadian Oil and Gas?" ad had the highest CTR but the worst conversion rate — a textbook message-to-landing-page mismatch. People clicked out of curiosity, but couldn't connect the ad framing to the platform's offering once they landed.

Key Findings
99.7%
Audience Network
Nearly all impressions served off-platform — through apps and third-party websites using LinkedIn's targeting data. Only $14 of $4,498 spend went to LinkedIn feed itself, generating 4 clicks and zero conversions.
2.65×
Avg Frequency
Each unique person in the 50,000-strong audience saw the campaign an average of 2.65 times. Reasonable frequency — but much of that exposure happened on low-attention placements where intent is low.
28%
View-Through Conversions
244 of 870 conversions are view-through attributions — people who saw the ad but never clicked, then converted via another path. LinkedIn's attribution model claims credit for these. Real intent signal is lower.
$3.47
Lowest Cost per Request
The "Invest in Canada with Confidence" creative delivered conversions at $3.47 each — 2.8× cheaper than the ad that received the most budget. The best-performing creative got the least spend.

What Didn't Work

Three structural issues explain the gap between strong headline numbers and full campaign potential.

1
Budget allocated inversely to performance
The "Investing in Canadian Oil and Gas?" creative received 55% of total spend ($2,476) but delivered the worst cost per conversion at $9.64 — nearly 3× the cost of the best-performing ad. The most efficient creative received only $1,557.
Fix → Pause or cap "Investing in Canadian Oil and Gas?" spend. Reallocate to "Invest in Canada with Confidence" and test scaling "Don't Get Left Behind" which had the lowest absolute CPC at $2.84.
2
99.7% Audience Network — effectively a display campaign
LinkedIn feed received $14 and generated zero conversions. The Audience Network is cheaper per impression but reaches people browsing unrelated apps with minimal purchase intent. For a high-consideration B2B product, this matters.
Fix → Shift 30–40% of budget to LinkedIn feed placements. Expect CPM to increase 3–4× but anticipate meaningfully higher post-demo conversion rates on sales calls.
3
Seniority targeting too broad — buying researchers, not buyers
Entry and Senior levels make up 68% of impressions. These audiences convert at 28–29% on the demo form, but are unlikely to hold budget authority. Directors, VPs, and CXOs — who click at higher rates — share just 20% of impressions.
Fix → Add Director, VP, and CXO seniority exclusions to entry-level audience. Run parallel campaigns targeting each seniority tier separately to measure pipeline quality against CPL.
4
Conversion tracking went live 10 days late
The LinkedIn Insight Tag conversion event wasn't active for the first 10 days of the campaign (early campaign period). All spend from that period — roughly $2,100 — has zero conversion attribution. True CPC is likely lower than $5.17.
Fix → Validate conversion tracking fires correctly before any future campaign launch. Add a pre-launch QA checklist including test conversion events.
5
Industry targeting pulling non-buyers
Technology and Business Consulting together received 54,000+ impressions — industries that are adjacent but unlikely to be direct buyers of a Canadian O&G investment analytics tool. Houston's strong conversion rate (26.7%) reinforces that oil & gas geography matters more than assumed.
Fix → Tighten industry targeting to Capital Markets, Credit Intermediation, Oil & Gas, and Financial Services. Increase Houston and Dallas–Fort Worth geo-bid adjustments.
What to Do Next
01
Reallocate to the winning creative
Move 70%+ of creative budget to "Invest in Canada with Confidence." At $3.47 per request, doubling its budget to $3,000 CAD projects ~865 additional conversions — more than the entire campaign generated.
Highest ROI change
02
Test LinkedIn feed placements
Allocate 30% of budget to LinkedIn feed only. Measure post-demo close rates against Audience Network conversions. If feed-sourced leads close at 2× the rate, the higher CPL is justified.
Quality test
03
Separate seniority tiers
Run three parallel campaigns: Entry/Senior, Director/Manager, and CXO/VP. Use identical creative but measure pipeline quality (SQLs, closed deals) per tier to find where real buyers are, not just form fillers.
Pipeline quality
04
Double down on Houston & DC
Houston converts at 26.7% and Washington DC at 29.1% — both punching above their impression weight. Increase geo-bid adjustments for energy corridor cities: Houston, Calgary, Denver, and Midland.
Geo optimisation
05
Fix tracking before next launch
A 10-day tracking gap cost visibility into ~$2,100 of spend. Implement a pre-launch QA protocol: test the Insight Tag, fire a test conversion event, and confirm attribution windows are configured correctly.
Data integrity
06
Narrow industry targeting
Remove Technology and Business Consulting from the audience. Add explicit company lists for top target accounts (JPMorgan, Wells Fargo, Bank of America, Morgan Stanley) using LinkedIn's Account Targeting feature.
Audience quality

Bottom line: This campaign proved the concept. $5.17 per conversion in B2B financial services is genuinely competitive, and a 1.99% CTR signals that the proposition resonates with the target audience. The core product is working.

The opportunity ahead is about quality, not volume — shifting from the broadest possible reach to the most qualified possible buyer. The data to make that shift is already here.
